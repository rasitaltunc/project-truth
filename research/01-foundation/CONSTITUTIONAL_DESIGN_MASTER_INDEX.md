## CRITICAL DECISION POINTS

### Decision 1: Do We Adopt Principle-Based Governance?
**Evidence:** Research document shows principle-based governance works (Wikipedia, Signal)
**Tradeoff:** Less precise than rule-based, but more adaptable
**Constitutional Reality:** Article I (5 core principles) + Article II (5 operational rules)
**Recommendation:** YES — principles guide long-term stability; rules handle specifics

---

### Decision 2: Do We Protect Sources Absolutely or Flexibly?
**Evidence:** Tor's absolute "no backdoors"; Wikimedia's governance by negotiation
**Tradeoff:** Absolute protection is rigid but trustworthy; flexible is adaptable but risky
**Constitutional Reality:** Principle 2 (Source Protection) is immutable; Rule 2 (Source Anonymity) is bright-line
**Recommendation:** YES — sources are sacred. This is non-negotiable.

---

### Decision 3: Do We Allow Community or Board to Decide Major Issues?
**Evidence:** Apache (meritocracy) vs. Wikimedia (negotiation) vs. Wikipedia (community consensus)
**Tradeoff:** Community is inclusive but slow; board is fast but elitist
**Constitutional Reality:** Article III provides three structures: Community Council, Board, Appeals Panel
**Recommendation:** YES — layered governance. Community for strategy; board for operations; appeals for justice

---

### Decision 4: How Hard Should Amendment Be?
**Evidence:** U.S. Constitution (too hard); many bylaws (too easy)
**Tradeoff:** Hard amendment prevents erosion but prevents adaptation; easy amendment allows growth but enables capture
**Constitutional Reality:** Rules need 2/3 board + 50% community; principles are immutable
**Recommendation:** YES — difficult amendment is feature, not bug. Prevents reactive changes

---

### Decision 5: Do We Need a Philosophy Booklet?
**Evidence:** Federalist Papers guide U.S. Constitution interpretation 200+ years later
**Tradeoff:** Time investment to build; huge payoff in institutional memory
**Constitutional Reality:** Philosophy booklet is separate but linked
**Recommendation:** YES — this is how Truth survives founder departure and ensures institutional coherence

## FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

### Q: Isn't this constitution too long?
**A:** Compare to real constitutions:
- U.S. Constitution: 4,000 words
- Indian Constitution: 145,000 words
- Truth Constitution: 8,000 words
We're between extremes. The philosophy booklet makes it understandable.

### Q: What if the board and community disagree?
**A:** Article II, Rule 5: Amendment passes after 6 months if both still disagree. Legitimacy takes time.

### Q: Can we change the core principles?
**A:** No. They're immutable. That's by design. They define "being Truth."

### Q: What if the constitution is wrong?
**A:** Then amend it. The amendment process exists for exactly this. But make it hard (intentionally).

### Q: How do I actually use this in decisions?
**A:** See Philosophy Booklet Guide, Part IV: Decision-Making Guide. Flowchart shows which principle applies.

### Q: Who's responsible if things go wrong?
**A:** Multiple layers (Article III governance structure) ensure no single person has unchecked power. Transparency ensures accountability.
